**Natural Language:**
THEOREM: The composition of two polynomial-time computable functions is also polynomial-time computable. Specifically, if f: Σ* → Σ* and g: Σ* → Σ* are polynomial-time computable functions, then the function h: Σ* → Σ* defined by h(x) = f(g(x)) is also polynomial-time computable.

PROOF SKETCH: 
1. Recall the definition of polynomial-time computable functions: a function f is polynomial-time computable if there exists a polynomial p such that for all inputs x, the computation time of f(x) is bounded by p(|x|), where |x| denotes the length of x.
2. Given f and g are polynomial-time computable, let p_f and p_g be their respective bounding polynomials.
3. The composition h(x) = f(g(x)) involves first computing g(x), which takes time at most p_g(|x|), and then computing f on the result, which takes time at most p_f(|g(x)|).
4. Since |g(x)| ≤ p_g(|x|) (as the output length of a polynomial-time function is polynomially bounded), the time to compute f(g(x)) is at most p_f(p_g(|x|)).
5. The overall time complexity of h is thus bounded by the polynomial p_g(|x|) + p_f(p_g(|x|)), which is still a polynomial in |x|.
6. Therefore, h is polynomial-time computable.

LEAN HINT:
- Imports: `import Complexity.Basic`
- Tactics: `apply`, `existsi`, `simp`, `omega` (for polynomial inequalities)
- Key Definitions: `polynomial_time_computable`, `polynomial.comp`

**Natural Language:**
THEOREM: The composition of two polynomial functions is also a polynomial function. Specifically, if f: ℕ → ℕ is a polynomial function of degree m and g: ℕ → ℕ is a polynomial function of degree n, then the composition h(x) = f(g(x)) is a polynomial function of degree m*n.

PROOF SKETCH: 
1. **Definition Recall**: Start by recalling the definition of a polynomial function, emphasizing that the degree of a polynomial is the highest power of the variable with a non-zero coefficient.
2. **Composition Analysis**: Consider the composition h(x) = f(g(x)). If g is a polynomial of degree n, then g(x) can be expressed as a sum of terms up to x^n. Similarly, f being a polynomial of degree m means f(y) can be expressed as a sum of terms up to y^m.
3. **Substitution**: Substitute g(x) into f(y). This substitution will result in terms where the highest degree term in g(x) (i.e., x^n) is raised to the power of m, yielding x^(m*n).
4. **Degree Determination**: Argue that the highest degree term in the composed function h(x) will indeed be x^(m*n), with lower-degree terms not affecting the overall degree of the polynomial.
5. **Formal Proof**: Use algebraic manipulation and properties of exponents to demonstrate that the degree of h(x) is the product of the degrees of f and g, thus proving the composition is a polynomial of degree m*n.

LEAN HINT:
- Imports: `import algebra.polynomial`
- Tactics: `ring`, `simp`, `degree_add_le`, `degree_mul_eq`, `by repeat {apply le_antisymm; simp [degree_le]}`

DIFFICULTY: Medium

**Lean 4 Proof:**
```lean
import Mathlib
import algebra.polynomial

variable (R : Type) [CommSemiring R]

theorem degree_mul_eq (p q : R[X]) (h : p ≠ 0) (h' : q ≠ 0) : 
  degree (p * q) = degree p + degree q := 
by simp [degree_mul, h, h']

theorem polynomial_composition_degree (f g : R[X]) (m n : ℕ) 
  (hf : degree f = m) (hg : degree g = n) : 
  degree (f ∘ g) = m * n := 
begin
  -- Assume f and g are non-zero polynomials
  assume hf' : f ≠ 0,
  assume hg' : g ≠ 0,

  -- Express g as a sum of terms up to x^n
  let g_x := finset.sum (finset.range (n + 1)) (λ i, (coeff g i) * X^i),

  -- Express f as a sum of terms up to y^m
  let f_y := finset.sum (finset.range (m + 1)) (λ i, (coeff f i) * Y^i),

  -- Substitute g into f
  let h_x := f_y ∘ g_x,

  -- Simplify the composition
  simp [h_x, f_y, g_x],

  -- Apply the degree multiplication property
  apply degree_mul_eq,
  simp [hf'],
  simp [hg'],
  simp [hf, hg],

  -- Repeat the process for the remaining terms
  by repeat {apply le_antisymm; simp [degree_le]},

  -- Establish the degree equality
  simp [degree_add_le, hf, hg],
  ring,
end
Note: `R[X]` denotes the polynomial ring over a commutative semiring `R`. The `degree` function returns the degree of a polynomial. The `coeff` function returns the coefficient of a term in a polynomial. `X` and `Y` are the variables of the polynomials `g` and `f`, respectively. The `∘` operator denotes the composition of polynomials.
```
