THEOREM: The sum of the lengths of two lists is equal to the length of their concatenation.

PROOF SKETCH: 
1. Consider two arbitrary lists `l1` and `l2`.
2. By definition, the length of a list is the number of elements in it.
3. The concatenation of `l1` and `l2`, denoted `l1 ++ l2`, appends all elements of `l2` to the end of `l1`.
4. The length of `l1 ++ l2` is the total count of elements from both `l1` and `l2`.
5. Thus, `length(l1) + length(l2) = length(l1 ++ l2)` by basic arithmetic and the definition of list concatenation.

THEOREM: For any two lists of natural numbers, the length of their concatenation is equal to the sum of their individual lengths.

PROOF SKETCH: This theorem can be proven by induction on the structure of the lists. Specifically, we can use structural induction on the first list, considering the base case where the first list is empty, and the inductive step where the first list is non-empty (i.e., a cons of a head element and a tail list). In the base case, the concatenation of an empty list and the second list is just the second list, so their lengths are trivially equal. In the inductive step, we assume the property holds for the tail list and show it holds for the entire list by using the definition of concatenation and the fact that adding an element to the front of a list increments its length by 1.

THEOREM: The composition of two polynomial-time computable functions is also polynomial-time computable.

PROOF SKETCH:
To prove this, we consider two functions f and g that are polynomial-time computable. We need to show that their composition, h(x) = f(g(x)), is also polynomial-time computable. 
1. Since f is polynomial-time computable, there exists a polynomial p such that the time complexity of f is bounded by p(|x|), where |x| denotes the size of the input x.
2. Similarly, for g, there exists a polynomial q such that the time complexity of g is bounded by q(|x|).
3. The composition h(x) = f(g(x)) involves first computing g(x) and then computing f on the result. 
4. The time complexity of computing g(x) is bounded by q(|x|), and the size of the output of g(x) is at most q(|x|) (assuming the output size is polynomially bounded by the input size, which is a reasonable assumption for polynomial-time computable functions).
5. Then, computing f on the result of g(x) takes time bounded by p(q(|x|)), since the input size for f is now the size of g(x)'s output.
6. Therefore, the total time complexity of h(x) is q(|x|) + p(q(|x|)), which is still a polynomial in |x|, specifically the polynomial being the sum of q and the composition of p with q.
