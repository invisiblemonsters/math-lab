**Natural Language:**
THEOREM: For all natural numbers n and m, the length of the list obtained by appending two lists of lengths n and m is equal to n + m.

PROOF SKETCH: 
We can prove this by induction on the structure of lists. For the base case, appending two empty lists results in an empty list, and 0 + 0 = 0. For the inductive step, consider two lists, one of length n and the other of length m. Appending them will result in a list where the first element is followed by the rest of the first list and then the entire second list. By the definition of list length, this will be 1 + (n - 1 + m) = n + m, which simplifies to the desired result.

LEAN HINT:
- Imports: `import Data.List`
- Tactics: `induction`, `simp [List.length_append]`, `norm_num`

DIFFICULTY: easy

**Lean 4 Proof:**
```lean

import Data.List

theorem length_append (α : Type) (as bs : List α) : 
  (as ++ bs).length = as.length + bs.length := 
by induction as 
  case nil => simp [List.length_append, List.length_nil]
  case cons a as hi => simp [List.length_append, List.length_cons] at *
    exact Nat.succ_eq_add_one (hi)
```

**Natural Language:**
THEOREM: For any list of natural numbers, the sum of the elements of the list is equal to the sum of the elements of its reverse.

PROOF SKETCH: 
To prove this, we can use induction on the length of the list. For the base case, an empty list has a sum of 0, and its reverse (also empty) has the same sum. For the inductive step, assume the property holds for all lists of length n. Consider a list of length n+1. The sum of the original list is the head plus the sum of the tail. The reverse of the list is the reverse of the tail appended with the head. By the inductive hypothesis, the sum of the tail is equal to the sum of its reverse. Therefore, the sum of the original list is equal to the sum of its reverse.

**Natural Language:**
THEOREM: The composition of two polynomial-time computable functions is also polynomial-time computable.

PROOF SKETCH: 
To prove this, we consider two polynomial-time computable functions f and g. By definition, there exist polynomials p and q such that the time complexity of f is bounded by p and the time complexity of g is bounded by q. We then analyze the time complexity of the composition h(x) = f(g(x)). The time taken by h(x) is the sum of the time taken by g(x) and the time taken by f applied to the result of g(x). Since the size of g(x) is bounded by a polynomial in the size of x (due to the polynomial-time computability of g), the time taken by f(g(x)) is bounded by p(q(|x|)). Thus, the total time complexity of h(x) is bounded by q(|x|) + p(q(|x|)), which is itself a polynomial in |x|. Therefore, the composition h is polynomial-time computable.

- Imports: `import Data.Polynomial` and relevant complexity theory definitions from Mathlib (or your own developing library).
- Tactics: `apply`, `use` (for polynomial construction), `simp` for polynomial expressions, possibly `induction` on polynomial terms if necessary.

-- Polynomial-time computable function definition
def polyTimeComputable (f : ℕ → ℕ) : Prop :=
  ∃ p : ℕ[X], ∀ x, f x ≤ eval x p

-- Composition of two polynomial-time computable functions
theorem compositionPolyTimeComputable :
  ∀ (f g : ℕ → ℕ), polyTimeComputable f → polyTimeComputable g → polyTimeComputable (f ∘ g) :=
begin
  intro f g hf hg,
  obtain ⟨p, hp⟩ := hf,

  intro x,
  simp only [Function.comp_apply, eval_add, eval_comp],
  apply Nat.add_le_add,
  exact hq x,
  apply hp,
  exact hq x,
end
